# DCU Guide to Writing Learning Outcomes (July 2023)

Produced by the DCU Teaching Enhancement Unit (TEU). 12 pages.

## Definition

> "Learning outcomes are statements that specify what a learner is expected to know and be able to do with that knowledge upon successful completion of a course of learning."

At module level:

> "a learning outcome is an **observable** statement of what the learner is expected to be able to do on successful completion of the module in order to demonstrate their knowledge, understanding, and skills."

## Standard Stem

- Module level: **"Upon successful completion of the module, students will be able to…"**
- Programme level: **"Upon successful completion of the programme, a student will be able to…"**

## LO Formula (from Macquarie University)

`Action Verb + Content or Topic to be Learned + Context = Learning Outcome That Is Assessable`

| Action Verb | Content/Topic | Context |
|---|---|---|
| Identify | ethical issues | in home care settings |
| Evaluate | alternative solutions to | specified marketing problems |
| Apply | the core concepts of X and Y theories | to economic analyses |
| Write | logical and concise reports | appropriate to accounting practice |
| Demonstrate | effective communication and presentation skills to convey | technical information to a non-expert audience |

## Golden Rules (verbatim)

> Learning outcomes should:
> - Always start with an action verb.
> - Use one verb for each learning outcome.
> - Be observable and/or measurable.
> - Be expressed clearly in plain English so that they can be understood by students, colleagues and external organisations.
> - Describe the kind of performance/standard expected.
> - Be appropriate for the level of learning (e.g., undergraduate 1st year, postgraduate).
> - Be limited in number (**ideally 4–6 per module**) while including all significant elements of the module.

## Common Problems (verbatim)

> - Use of the verb **'Understand'** — this is not an observable or measurable output so is not appropriate for module learning outcomes in particular.
> - Learning outcomes do not align with assessment.
> - **Overuse of lower-level action verbs** such as 'identify', 'describe' and 'explain'. In a higher education setting there should be a mix of Bloom's levels of learning evident that are appropriate to the NFQ level of the module or programme.
> - **Overly wordy/unclear learning outcomes** — try to keep them as short and succinct as possible.

## Self-Assessment Checklist (from UCD/Surgenor 2010)

- Does each begin with an action verb?
- Have they been stated in observable and measurable terms?
- Are words such as "know", "understand" avoided?
- Do they address a variety of cognitive levels?
- Are outcomes at the appropriate level?
- Do all the outcomes fit within the aims and content of the module?
- Are they realistic and achievable in context?
- Are they clear to staff and students?
- Do they align with the programme outcomes?

## Bloom's Taxonomy Position

DCU uses Bloom's as a reference tool but explicitly qualifies it:

> "Note: Bloom's Taxonomy is a common but somewhat contested verb-based framework which suggests that certain verbs correspond to specific 'levels' of cognitive skill. While useful as a reference tool and thesaurus, we **do not recommend exclusive use** of this taxonomy when writing learning outcomes as the precise context of each verb has an important bearing on cognitive demands (Stanny, 2016)."

Verb mix across levels is required: "In a higher education setting there should be a mix of Bloom's levels of learning evident that are appropriate to the NFQ level of the module or programme."

## QQI/NFQ Relationship

DCU explicitly grounds LO requirements in QQI's Core Statutory Quality Assurance Guidelines. Programme LOs must align with the NFQ grid of level indicators. DCU treats NFQ/QQI as a floor and adds its own supplementary guidance above it (Macquarie formula, golden rules, verb mix requirement, VASCULAR clarity principle).

- NFQ level alignment required
