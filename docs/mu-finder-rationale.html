Maynooth University — Open Day Tools

MU Programme Finder
Pedagogical Rationale

A note on the design philosophy, theoretical grounding, and academic defensibility of the three guided pathways offered by the MU Programme Finder.

Prof. John Keating · Maynooth University · 2026

1. Purpose and Context

The MU Programme Finder is a browser-based tool designed for use at Open Days and on the Maynooth University website. It addresses a specific problem: an incoming student arrives facing 55 undergraduate programmes across three faculties and, frequently, no clear basis for choosing between them.

The tool does not make decisions for students. It surfaces information, structures self-reflection, and opens conversations. No account is required; nothing is recorded; no choice is locked in. It is a guided exploration environment, not a selection mechanism.

Three distinct pathways are offered, because students arrive with different starting points. A student who already knows they want to be a data scientist needs a different entry point from one who knows only their predicted Leaving Certificate grades, who in turn needs something different from one who has no strong direction at all.

2. The Three Pathways

🧠

Pathway 1 — Interests & Strengths Quiz

The student answers 9–12 short statements about what they enjoy and how they think. The tool scores responses across eight academic interest categories and returns a ranked list of programmes aligned with their profile.

📚

Pathway 2 — Leaving Certificate Subject Explorer

The student selects their LC subjects and grades. The tool calculates estimated CAO points and returns all 55 programmes sorted by achievability, showing which are within reach, borderline, or beyond current points.

🧭

Pathway 3 — Career Finder

The student selects a career or field from a curated list. The tool returns MU programmes ranked by how closely each aligns with the skills and knowledge areas relevant to that career. Where no direct match exists, broad entry degrees are recommended.

Each pathway is independent and non-exclusive. A student may use all three. The CAO points constraint from Pathway 2 always operates in the background: a student who discovers an interest via Pathway 1 or 3 is returned programme cards that include points thresholds, providing an immediate reality check.

3. The Interests & Strengths Quiz — Theoretical Basis

3.1 What it is — and what it is not

The quiz is an academic interest inventory. It asks what kinds of activities, problems, and ways of thinking a student enjoys. It does not measure personality traits, infer psychological types, or claim to identify latent characteristics the student is unaware of.

This distinction matters. The well-documented criticisms of tools such as the Myers–Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI) — poor test-retest reliability, false binary categories, limited predictive validity — apply to instruments that claim to measure stable personality constructs. The Interests & Strengths Quiz makes no such claim. It asks the student to report, in the moment, what they enjoy. The student is the authority on that question.

The key distinction: measuring interests (what activities engage and motivate you) is fundamentally different from measuring personality (what kind of person you are). Interest inventories have a substantially stronger empirical record for educational and vocational guidance purposes.

3.2 Theoretical foundation: Holland's Person–Environment Fit

The theoretical backbone of the quiz is Holland's Theory of Vocational Personalities and Work Environments (Holland, 1959; 1997). Holland proposed that individuals can be characterised by their interest profile across six broad types (Realistic, Investigative, Artistic, Social, Enterprising, Conventional — the RIASEC model), and that environments (including academic programmes) can be similarly characterised. When an individual's interest profile is congruent with their environment, outcomes improve: higher satisfaction, stronger persistence, and better performance.

Holland's framework underpins the Strong Interest Inventory (SII), one of the most extensively validated psychometric instruments in existence, and the O*NET occupational database used by career services worldwide. The eight interest categories used in the MU quiz (Quantitative, Scientific, Computing, Creative, Language, Humanities, Social, Business) are a domain-specific mapping that is consistent with the RIASEC taxonomy and with the disciplinary structure of MU's three faculties.

3.3 Empirical support for interest-based guidance

Rounds and Su (2014) summarise decades of meta-analytic evidence: interest–occupation congruence is a significant predictor of job satisfaction, and interest profiles are among the most stable individual-difference measures from adolescence through early adulthood — far more stable than many personality trait measures. Tracey and Robbins (2006) specifically examine interest congruence and academic persistence, finding that students in programmes congruent with their interest profiles have higher retention rates.

For the higher education context, Cabrera and La Nasa (2000) and Tinto's integration theory (1987) both identify academic fit — the alignment between a student's dispositions and their programme's intellectual culture — as a significant predictor of persistence and completion. Using expressed interests as a proxy for fit is a reasonable and empirically grounded approach.

3.4 Responding to common objections

Objection Does it apply here?
MBTI and personality typing have poor reliability and limited validity Not applicable. The quiz measures expressed interests, not personality types. There are no binary categories, no trait inference, no typology.
Profiling can create self-limiting beliefs ("I'm not a science person") Mitigated by design. Results are framed as suggestions for exploration, not verdicts. The output is "these programmes are worth considering," not "you are this type."
High-stakes decisions should not be driven by a short quiz Not applicable. The quiz does not make decisions. The student retains full agency over their CAO choice. Points requirements provide an independent reality check.
The quiz has not been psychometrically validated on a normed sample Correct, and worth acknowledging. The appropriate bar for an open-day exploration tool is not the same as for a clinical instrument. The question is whether the tool is helpful and does not cause harm — both of which are satisfied.
Students may answer strategically (giving answers they think lead to a desired result) Possible, but this undermines the tool's usefulness to the student themselves, not to the institution. The app has no selection function.

3.5 Irish secondary school context

Irish students arrive at third-level having engaged with various forms of interest and aptitude assessment through their school guidance counsellors. Standardised instruments such as differential aptitude tests and vocational interest surveys are in routine use in Irish post-primary schools. The Interests & Strengths Quiz is consistent with this existing practice, and students are unlikely to find the format unfamiliar or inappropriate.

Crucially, students who distrust quiz-based tools or who arrive with a clear direction already in mind are not required to use Pathway 1. Pathways 2 and 3 are fully independent alternatives.

Framing note for colleagues: The Interests & Strengths Quiz is best understood as a structured conversation starter — a way of externalising and making explicit the informal self-reflection that students are already doing. At an Open Day, where one-to-one guidance is not feasible at scale, it offers a systematic and repeatable version of what a skilled guidance counsellor achieves in a 15-minute session.

4. The Leaving Certificate Subject Explorer — Design Rationale

This pathway addresses a concrete, practical need: Irish students applying through the CAO need to know whether they are likely to meet the points threshold for a given programme. Many are unaware that their subject choices and grade expectations translate directly into a points band, and that certain programmes have specific subject requirements (e.g. Mathematics for Engineering, a language for some Arts programmes).

The Subject Explorer does two things: it estimates the student's likely CAO points from self-reported grades, and it surfaces all 55 MU programmes sorted by how achievable they are relative to that estimate. This is a purely informational tool — no interpretation, no inference. Its defensibility is straightforward: it is a calculation engine and a filter, grounded in the published CAO points data for 2025 entry.

Its pedagogical value lies in expanding the decision space rather than narrowing it. Students frequently consider only three or four programmes and are unaware of others well within their reach. By sorting 55 programmes by achievability, the tool surfaces options the student may never have considered.

5. The Career Finder — Design Rationale

A significant proportion of students, particularly those from backgrounds with limited third-level exposure, approach programme choice primarily through the lens of career outcome: "What do I need to study to become X?" The Career Finder addresses this directly.

The tool maps 55 MU programmes against a curated set of career fields, grouped into nine categories. Each programme is tagged with primary and secondary academic interest categories; each career is tagged with the same taxonomy. Relevance is calculated from category overlap.

Where no MU programme directly prepares for a specific career (medicine, library science, veterinary), the tool makes this transparent rather than returning empty results. It explains honestly that MU does not offer a programme in that field, and suggests the broad entry degrees (BA MH101, BSc MH201) as foundational routes that keep multiple career options open.

A note on career-driven programme choice: Some colleagues express concern that framing a university degree primarily in terms of career outcomes instrumentalises education in ways inconsistent with liberal arts values. This is a legitimate and important concern. The Career Finder is offered as one of three pathways, not the only one, and it does not claim that a degree's value is reducible to its career destination. Students who find the career framing unhelpful can use Pathways 1 or 2 instead. The tool serves students who arrive with career-oriented questions; it does not impose a career-oriented framing on students who do not.

6. Shared Design Principles

  • No account, no data collection. Everything runs in the browser. No personal data is transmitted or stored server-side. This removes any concern about student data being used for institutional purposes beyond the student's intent.
  • Non-exclusive pathways. A student who uses Pathway 1 and is unhappy with the results can immediately try Pathway 2 or 3. No pathway locks in a conclusion.
  • Transparent scoring. Match percentages and category tags are shown directly on programme cards, so students can inspect and challenge the logic rather than receiving a black-box verdict.
  • Points reality check. Every programme card shows the 2025 CAO points threshold, regardless of which pathway the student uses. This prevents aspirational results from being misleading.
  • Human agency preserved. The tool never says "you should apply for this programme." It says "this programme is worth considering." The CAO form remains entirely the student's decision.
